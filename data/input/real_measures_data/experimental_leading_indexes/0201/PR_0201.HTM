March 7, 2002

Stock and Watson Indicator Report

January 2002


The Experimental Index of Coincident Indicators (XCI) fell to 237.1 in January from its December value of 237.5, based on the most recently available data.  During the three months from October to January the XCI fell by 0.8% at an annual rate.  During the previous three months from July to October, the XCI fell by 3.9% at an annual rate.  Over the entire six months from July to January, the XCI therefore fell 2.4% at an annual rate.


A calculation based on the variables in the XCI indicates that the probability that the economy was still in recession in January is 43%.  Historical values of this Experimental Concident Recession Index (XRI-C) are shown in Figure 4.


The Experimental Leading Index (XLI) for January is 4.1.  This index, based on seven leading indicators, is a forecast of the percentage growth of the XCI (at an annual rate) over the next six months, from January to July 2002.


Of the indicators used to calculate the XLI, four made a positive contribution to the index, relative to trend: housing authorizations (building permits); part-time employment due to slack work;  the premium on 3-month commercial paper over 3-month U.S. Treasury bills;  and the spread between the yield on 10-year vs. 1-year U.S. Treasury bonds.  Three of the indicators made a negative contribution to the index, relative to trend:  manufacturers' unfilled orders;  exchange rates;  and the yield on 10-year U.S. Treasury bonds.


Based on the most recent data, the Experimental Recession Index (XRI), which estimates the probability that the economy will be in a recession six months later, was 1% in January, indicating a 1% probability that the economy will be in a recession in July 2002.  The alternative experimental recession index (the XRI-2), which is based on seven leading indicators that do not include interest rates or interest rate spreads, estimates a 6% probability that the economy will be in a recession in July 2002.
